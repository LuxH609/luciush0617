Architectural TPMS Structures: A Sustainable Modular Prototype for Energy-Efficient Future Data Centers

ABSTRACT

In the digital age, the rising global demand for data centers underscores the urgent need for more efficient cooling systems. Although air cooling remains the most prevalent method in current data centers, it is hindered by high energy consumption and limited efficiency. To tackle these challenges, I propose a modular data center architecture prototype based on Triply Periodic Minimal Surface (TPMS) structures. By leveraging the exceptional heat exchange properties and structural advantages of TPMS, this design aims to significantly enhance cooling efficiency and reduce operational energy costs. In addition to its cooling benefits, this research explores the use of sustainable materials, such as bio-concrete and mycelium, combined with advanced digital prefabrication techniques, enabling efficient transportation and rapid on-site assembly, while lowering carbon emissions during construction. The proposed prototype addresses the increasing global demand for scalable, energy-efficient data center solutions. Furthermore, the research encourages the reuse of waste heat, promoting energy recycling and reducing environmental impact, all in alignment with global sustainability goals.


Keywords: data center, TPMS, computational design, modular architecture, cooling system, sustainability, digital fabrication, green material


RESEARCH BACKGROUND

Fueled by the rapid advancement of cutting-edge technologies like artificial intelligence (AI), big data, and cloud computing, data centers—centralized facilities for storing, managing, and processing vast amounts of data—have emerged as essential infrastructure in recent years (Dayarathna et al., 2016). Recent data from Synergy Research Group reveals that the number of large-scale data centers operated by hyperscale providers surpassed 1,000 in early 2024, doubling since 2017. Additionally, projections suggest that the total capacity of these data centers will double again within the next four years.

With the rapid expansion of data centers, managing the substantial heat generated by servers under high computational loads has become a critical challenge (Habibi Khalaj & Halgamuge, 2017). Thus, Efficient cooling systems are essential for stable operation of data centers. According to Uptime Institute's 2021 survey, cooling systems typically consume around 30% of a data center's total energy consumption, as reflected in the global average PUE of 1.57 in the report (2021 Data Center Industry Survey Results, n.d.) . Basically, there are three primary methods commonly used for cooling: air cooling, liquid cooling, and free cooling (Zhang et al., 2021) .

Air cooling, the most widely adopted approach, relies on air conditioning systems. Yet, it is limited by the low heat capacity of air, thus consuming a huge amount of power. As one of the alternatives, liquid cooling, which uses liquids to improve heat dissipation and energy efficiency with relatively lower energy usage, delivers better performance than air cooling. However, it is hindered by higher installation and maintenance costs and significantly higher potential for leakage rick (Zhang et al., 2021).

Considering the huge potential of energy saving, free cooling, which leverages natural sources such as cold air or water from the environment, offers significantly lower energy consumption. Intel research indicates that free cooling, combined with higher operating temperatures, can reduce cooling costs by up to 67%, potentially saving $2.87 million annually for a 10 MW data center. However, its effectiveness is highly dependent on geographical location and weather conditions (Lee & Chen, 2013), as demonstrated by Google's use of seawater for cooling in Finland and cold air in Ireland. Besides, it also faces challenges including increasing the IT equipment aging and damage due to air humidity and contamination (Sorell, 2007), which will increase the cost for sub-systems like dehumidification and filtration, as well as maintaining and cleaning cost for IT systems. Furthermore, it also requires a backup system using other cooling methods.
